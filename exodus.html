Exodus

May 24, 2026

It’s rare, or maybe it’s not rare, but I think it’s rare to make that leap from “colleagues” to “friends.” I deem the moment I’m spending time with a colleague on a weekend the turning point to where I can distinctly call out that we have crossed the sacred line leaping right into friendship.

I’ve been lucky to experience that conversion on more than one occasion in the past few years. Where the quarterly catch-ups turn into monthly coffee chats that then turn into “Hey I’m hosting a dinner this weekend” or “my partner is celebrating their birthday, you should stop by.” There are really a plethora of positive social cues that signal someone wants to see you outside of normal working hours. It’s so fun crossing that line and getting to experience someone in their day-to-day. You get to be brought into their personal world where no one has to talk about deals or termsheets or founders (unless they want to of course). Sometimes work talk inevitably happens, and other times you forget work is the common ground that brought you into the relationship in the first place.

As aforementioned, I’ve been lucky to call a few work gals my actual friends now. I send some voice memos, some invites to dinner, and some we get together to have a much needed cocktail. It’s truly so much fun to be surrounded by extremely intelligent and interesting gals. Lately though, the get togethers have all started the same way: Someone suggests catching up, a date is set, and then ten minutes into the conversation the infamous line bubbles to the surface:

“I’m leaving venture and joining a startup.”

This has happened 4 times in the last month. Each time I’m immediately a little sad and quickly put my emotions on hold to ask more questions. I learn as much as possible about my friend's next phase of work life, and the happiness I feel for them is genuine. The sadness that ensues as I’m seeing someone leaving the venture space, whom I started alongside, is also genuine, and being introduced to the new wave of VCs makes me feel outdated.

I completely understand why there are more people leaving the venture space for greener pastures on the operating side. VCs are throwing money at startups with valuations that make the 2020 era seem tame. You can get paid a whole lot more, earn equity, and if you really hate it you can leave in a year or so with a nice cushion of savings and likely a favorable vesting period on that equity. It makes sense! It does…but I’m still sad about it.

The first reason is completely selfish in that I always look inward in these conversations and feel a sense of fomo that I’m missing out on opportunities. That I’m not working at a startup to gain a tangible skill that I can then take with me to wherever my future employment holds. The second feeling is more interesting. I feel a sense of sadness that smart, capable, and inquisitive women are leaving the space altogether without having a sense of the personality or character of the person who will replace them. There is something melancholy about how many people, women specifically, are leaving venture.

I’ve heard from veterans that are smarter than myself that these exits are cyclical, so maybe my sadness is a little naive. I’m more so curious by what that means for the industry as a whole if so many intelligent people are flocking away from it with no intention to return any time soon. Which firms remain strong and which ones revert to the mean and lose the people who made their firms stand out in the first place.

I’ve griped about this before but there really aren’t a ton of senior women in venture. To be clear, I’ve never been one to be on board with mandates that state to “only invest in women founders” or make it seem like a charity case when they write a check for a woman. I am an advocate for women in leadership positions, though. I’m also an advocate for having a different opinion and bias in the room, which can be as simple as having a few more women at the table to balance out everyone else. Women having access and then choosing what to do with it.

But all the women are leaving long before their path to partner is cemented!

Losing all my friends, losing important voices in venture, it sucks.

I think when I look at who is beside me in the next five to ten years, I’ll be staring at the top people in venture, the ones who remained through all the churn. These women will be seasoned investors and likely the kind of people who know how to get access to a room and know how to stay in one.

As sad as I am to have watched my work friends, who became simply “friends”, leave the space, I’m excited to see who is still standing when the smoke clears.

Hopefully, I’ll be one of the lucky few. (“lucky” in this case is subjective)
